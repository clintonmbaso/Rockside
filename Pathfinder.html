Introduction
The requirements and teaching helps are taken from the North American Division (NAD) program of the same name. That program is intended for children four years old, with the Little Fish program for children five years old. Children should not start this class before they have passed their fourth birthday.
The NAD manual can be used if you wish to order them from Advent Source (see below). However the cost is fairly high especially when freight is added. The manuals are very colourful with lots of helpful information but they are NOT NECESSARY to run the course. Also it must be remembered that the requirements in this manual are NOT the same as those in the NAD manual. Almost all the material here is taken from that manual, but in most cases it is shortened and there are general articles of helps for leaders and teachers that are omitted here.

CAUTION
Some of these activities would not be considered suitable for Sabbath hours in most of our churches in Sydney. However, they have been listed as they are in the NAD manual. It may be that some of the Stars would be best done on a Sunday.

A story of the parable of the lost sheep, or of the childhood of Jesus (the visit to the Temple at age 12) would be especially suitable for this group.

Bring oversized T-shirts and head scarves so the children can dress up as in Bible times. Encourage the children to take off their shoes and socks, and pretend to cook, or watch their sheep etc. Or just do dress up letting the children try different ideas, and let them suggest the kinds of toys Jesus might have played with (hint - his father was a carpenter so there would have been wood scraps).

Draw an outline of a lamb, and prepare pictures of shepherds. Let the children glue cotton wool onto the lamb picture. While they are working or in a de-brief afterward discuss how a shepherd deals with a lost lamb and how Jesus deals with us when we wander from the way he wants us to go.
• With paddlepop sticks and white glue let the children build flat topped Bible land houses.
Talk about the fact that Jesus was a carpenter.
This is an Iranian game that can be played sitting in a circle, and could easily be the type of game that Jesus played with his friends. The children sit in a circle with a leader. The leader does an action and the children take turns round the circle copying the action (pull a face, tug on ear or nose, stroke the hair of the person next to you, etc). In the original game anyone who laughed or made a giggle or a noise was out, but let the children stay in though trying to exercise self-control.

Senses
Bring a variety of plants that are safe to touch and smell, especially ones with strong scents or unusual textures. Let the children touch and smell and discuss them.
Nature sounds
Rain using rainsticks (cardboard tubes with the ends sealed, with beans or rice inside. Thunder, drum sticks on a chair. Brook (a bottle with some water, then blow into it with a straw. Use a magnifying glass to look at the texture of different rocks. Scrape the rocks with a nail to see the different hardness of rocks. Weigh rocks of the same size but different densities on scales. Let the children sort the rocks by size using the different sized holes.
Rock sculptures
Glue small rocks to containers of cardboard or plastic, or other larger rocks. Encourage the Pre-Adventurers to make sculptures.

Here is an action rhyme that could be used for the song about families.
There are all kinds of families I see, Some are two and some are three (hold up two then three fingers) Some are eight and some are four (change the number of fingers showing) And some are more and more and more! (Raise and lower all 10 fingers.)
Do four activities in the Lamb Frolic time, including at least one craft and one activity, plus one discussion on the things the children have learnt about families. Here are three suggestions.
• Play house.
Hang sheets from a clothes line to make walls, have a dress up box, bits of furniture (TV, phone, and other props). Have the children act out different members of a family.
• Family Tree.
Draw an outline of a tree with large leaves. Each child will need to go to an adult to place names of the people in their family on their tree. Make sure the child’s name is on one of the leaves. (Nanna, Mummy, Daddy are suitable names, you do not have to use first names etc).
• Family matching.
Find pictures of parents, grand-parents, children etc (two of each!). On index cards, make 16 to 20 cards in a pack with 8 to 10 pairs of pictures. Have an adult with two to three children, to supervise playing game and discuss about families. Spread cards out face down, turn up two cards, if they match the child keeps them. As the child takes a matching pair ask what family member would this be (mother, sister etc). The NAD manual suggests www.gettyimages.com but going to Google, and selecting images, the father, mother, boy girl etc may be quicker.

G1MathT1L01 – Introduction to Counting and Number Recognition
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Counting and Number Recognition

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will be able to count objects from 1 to 10, recognize and identify numbers from 1 to 10, and associate numerals with their written form.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Number cards (1-10)
- Counting objects (e.g., small toys, blocks, or pictures)
- Whiteboard and markers

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing what counting is. Explain that counting is the process of determining the quantity of items.
2. Introduce the concept of numbers as symbols that represent quantities. Show them a number card (e.g., "1") and explain that it represents one item.
3. Emphasize the importance of numbers in our daily lives, such as when we tell time or count items.

Activity 1: Counting Objects (10 minutes):
1. Distribute a set of objects (toys, blocks, etc.) to each student.
2. Ask the students to count the objects in their set, starting from 1 and going up to 10.
3. Encourage them to say the numbers aloud as they count the objects.

Activity 2: Number Recognition (10 minutes):
1. Show the students a number card (e.g., "3").
2. Ask them to identify the number and show that many fingers.
3. Repeat this with several number cards, gradually increasing the complexity.

Activity 3: Matching Numbers and Objects (10 minutes):
1. Place a random number of objects (1-10) on a table.
2. Show the students a number card and ask them to find the corresponding number of objects on the table.
3. This activity reinforces the association between numbers and quantities.

Activity 4: Writing Numerals (5 minutes):
1. Have the students practice writing numerals on their individual whiteboards.
2. Start with simpler numbers like 1 and 2 and gradually progress to more complex numbers.
3. Encourage them to write the numerals neatly.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Summarize the lesson by revisiting what counting is and how numbers represent quantities.
2. Remind students to practice counting and recognizing numbers in their daily activities.
3. Distribute number cards or worksheets for additional practice at home.

Homework:
- Provide a worksheet with pictures of objects and ask the students to count them and write the corresponding numerals.

Assessment:
- Monitor students' participation in activities and check their homework for understanding of counting and number recognition.

Adaptations:
- For students with special needs, provide additional visual aids or one-on-one support during activities.
- For advanced students, introduce numbers beyond 10 and challenge them with more complex counting activities.

G1MathT1L02 – Counting Forward and Backward
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Counting Forward and Backward using the Number Line

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will be able to count forward from 1 to 20 and backward from 10 to 1 using a number line. They will understand the concept of a number line as a visual representation of counting numbers.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Large number line chart (1 to 20)
- Small number lines for each student (1 to 20)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Number cards (1-20)

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by introducing the concept of counting forward and backward using a number line.
2. Explain that a number line is a visual tool with numbers placed at equal intervals. It helps us see the order of numbers.
3. Show the large number line chart and point out that 1 is on the left end, and numbers increase as you move to the right.

Activity 1: Counting Forward (15 minutes):
1. Distribute small number lines to each student. These should have the numbers 1 to 20 printed on them.
2. Ask students to stand in a circle and start at number 1.
3. One by one, have each student say the next number in the sequence, continuing up to 20. Encourage them to use their number lines for reference.

Activity 2: Counting Backward (15 minutes):
1. Using the whiteboard, draw a simple number line from 10 to 1.
2. Explain that counting backward is like going in the opposite direction on the number line.
3. Have the students take turns saying the numbers in reverse order, starting from 10 and ending at 1.

Activity 3: Number Line Exploration (5 minutes):
1. Show the large number line chart again and ask students to point to different numbers.
2. Ask them questions like, "What comes after 7?" or "What's two numbers before 15?"
3. This activity reinforces their understanding of the number line's organization.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Summarize the lesson by reviewing the key points about counting forward and backward on a number line.
2. Emphasize the importance of number lines in helping us understand the order of numbers.

Homework:
- Assign homework where students have to complete number sequences both forward and backward using their small number lines.

Assessment:
- Observe students' participation in activities to ensure they can count forward and backward using the number line.
- Review their completed homework to assess their understanding.

Adaptations:
- Provide additional support and guidance for students who may struggle with counting forward and backward.
- Challenge advanced students by introducing larger numbers on the number line or skipping numbers in the sequence.

G1MathT1L03 – Patterns in Counting
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Patterns in Counting

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will be able to identify and understand simple counting patterns, such as counting by twos or fives, and recognize repeating patterns in the ones place of numbers.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Number cards (1-10)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Small objects or counters

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing the concept of patterns in counting.
2. Explain that patterns are sequences of numbers that follow a particular rule or order.
3. Mention that today, you will explore some simple counting patterns.

Activity 1: Counting by Twos (15 minutes):
1. Start by counting with the class from 1 to 10.
2. After reaching 10, introduce the concept of counting by twos.
3. Count together by twos, starting from 2, and continue up to 20.
4. Encourage students to observe the pattern and recognize that they're skipping every other number.

Activity 2: Counting by Fives (10 minutes):
1. Count together from 1 to 10 again.
2. After reaching 10, introduce counting by fives.
3. Count by fives, starting from 5 and going up to 25.
4. Help students notice the pattern of adding 5 each time.

Activity 3: Identifying Ones Place Patterns (10 minutes):
1. Display random numbers on the whiteboard, such as 12, 23, 34, and 45.
2. Ask students to focus on the ones place (the digit in the rightmost position).
3. Discuss how this digit changes from 1 to 9, and then repeats the pattern.
4. Practice identifying this pattern by displaying more numbers.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Summarize the lesson by reviewing the two types of counting patterns learned: counting by twos and fives and recognizing ones place patterns.
2. Explain that recognizing patterns can help us count and understand numbers more easily.

Homework:
- Assign homework where students have to identify and write down patterns in numbers they find in everyday life (e.g., from street addresses, phone numbers, or calendars).

Assessment:
- Assess students' understanding of counting patterns by observing their participation during the activities.
- Review their homework to check if they can identify patterns in real-world numbers.

Adaptations:
- Provide additional examples for students who need more practice in recognizing patterns.
- Challenge advanced students to identify and explain more complex counting patterns in numbers.

G1MathT1L04 – Ordinal Numbers
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Ordinal Numbers

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will be able to understand and use ordinal numbers, such as first, second, third, etc., in various contexts.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Flashcards with ordinal numbers (first, second, third, etc.)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Small objects for activities

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by explaining the concept of ordinal numbers. Emphasize that ordinal numbers tell us the position or order of things.
2. Write the words "first," "second," and "third" on the whiteboard and discuss how they are different from regular numbers.

Activity 1: Understanding Ordinal Numbers (10 minutes):
1. Show flashcards with ordinal numbers to the class, starting with "first" and going up to at least "tenth."
2. Have students repeat the words and discuss their meanings. For example, "first" means the thing in the front, "second" is the one after the first, and so on.

Activity 2: Ordinal Numbers in Action (15 minutes):
1. Create a simple scenario where students are "lining up" for a school activity or game.
2. Ask students to stand in a line, and then call out students one by one, assigning them ordinal numbers, such as "You are the first in line," "You are the second in line," and so on.
3. Repeat the process a few times, changing the order each time, so students understand the concept better.

Activity 3: Ordinal Number Objects (10 minutes):
1. Distribute small objects or counters to students.
2. Instruct students to arrange the objects in different orders and label them with ordinal numbers. For example, they might put the objects in a line and label them "third," "first," "second," etc.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Summarize the lesson by reinforcing the use and meaning of ordinal numbers.
2. Discuss real-life situations where we use ordinal numbers, such as races (1st place, 2nd place) or ordering at a restaurant (1st order, 2nd order).

Homework:
- Assign homework where students have to find and write down examples of ordinal numbers in books or magazines.

Assessment:
- Evaluate students' understanding of ordinal numbers during the activities.
- Review their homework to check if they can identify and use ordinal numbers in context.

Adaptations:
- Provide additional examples and practice for students who need it.
- Challenge advanced students by introducing more complex ordinal numbers, like "fifteenth" or "twenty-first."

G1MathT1L05 – Introduction to Addition
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Introduction to Addition

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will have a basic understanding of addition, be able to use the "+" symbol to represent addition, and grasp the concept of combining numbers.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Counters or small objects (e.g., buttons, blocks)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Number flashcards (0-10)

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Start by introducing the concept of addition. Explain that addition is a mathematical operation that combines two or more numbers to find the total or sum.
2. Write the "+" symbol on the whiteboard and explain that it represents addition.

Activity 1: Hands-On Addition (15 minutes):
1. Distribute a set of counters or small objects to each student.
2. Demonstrate adding two numbers using the counters. For example, place 2 counters and then add 3 more, showing that 2 + 3 equals 5.
3. Have students practice combining different sets of counters to find the sum.

Activity 2: Using Number Flashcards (15 minutes):
1. Show a number flashcard with a simple addition problem, e.g., 4 + 1.
2. Ask a student to come to the board and place the correct number of counters to represent the numbers in the problem.
3. Help the student count the total by combining the counters.
4. Repeat with other addition flashcards, involving multiple students.

Activity 3: Introduction to the "+" Symbol (10 minutes):
1. Write simple addition problems on the whiteboard using the "+" symbol. For example, 3 + 2 = ?
2. Ask students to take turns coming to the board to solve the problems by using counters or drawings to represent the numbers.
3. Encourage the class to say the problem aloud, place the counters, and find the answer together.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Summarize the lesson by reviewing the key points: the meaning of addition, the use of the "+" symbol, and how to combine numbers to find the sum.
2. Emphasize that addition helps us solve everyday problems, like adding up items or counting money.

Homework:
- Assign homework that includes simple addition problems using numbers from 0 to 10. Students can use counters, drawings, or pictures to find the answers.

Assessment:
- Observe students' participation in activities to ensure they understand the basics of addition.
- Review their homework to check if they can solve simple addition problems independently.

Adaptations:
- Provide additional practice for students who need it, using more or fewer counters depending on their skill level.
- Challenge advanced students with more complex addition problems involving larger numbers.

G1MathT1L06 – Adding Single-Digit Numbers
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Adding Single-Digit Numbers

Objective: By the end of this lesson, students will be able to add single-digit numbers from 0 to 9, practice addition facts, and understand the commutative property of addition.

Duration: 45 minutes

Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Number flashcards (0-9)
- Counters or small objects (e.g., buttons, coins)
- Worksheet with addition problems

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing the concept of adding single-digit numbers. Explain that we will be learning how to add numbers from 0 to 9.
2. Mention that addition is a way to combine numbers and find the total or sum.

Activity 1: Addition Facts (15 minutes):
1. Show number flashcards one at a time (e.g., 3) and ask the students to call out the addition facts for that number (e.g., 3 + 0, 3 + 1, 3 + 2, etc.).
2. Discuss how these addition facts represent different ways to add the same number.

Lesson 3: The Written Word

1. The Scriptures
The special revealings of divine truth have come to men by the prophets and, in a fuller way, by the appearance of the Son of God among men. (Heb. 1:1,2.) Apart from the Scriptures we would have no way of ascertaining either the messages of the prophets or the facts connected with Christ’s life and teaching. We look upon the Scriptures, therefore, as the written record of God’s revelations to the human race.

2. The contents of the Scriptures
The Scriptures (from the Latin scriptura, which, like the Greek graphai, means “writings”) come to us in the form of 66 books. This collection of writings has come to be known as the Bible, from the Greek word biblos, meaning “book.”

3. Their date of composition
The earliest writer of Scripture was Moses, who wrote about 1,500 years before Christ. Therefore the 39 books of the Old Testament were written over a period of one thousand years. The 27 books of the New Testament were all written before the close of the first century A.D., with the last, the book of Revelation, being written by John the apostle about A.D. 95. Thus, the writing of the books which constitute the Bible covered a period of approximately 1,600 years.

4. Their authorship In the main it was written by some 35 authors, but these men doubtless secured information and used documents written by many other writers.

5. Their arrangement
The order of the books in the Hebrew Bible of Christ’s day was not identical with their arrangement in our Bible. There were three main divisions of the Hebrew Bible, as follows:
i. The Law Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy.
ii. The Prophets Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and The Twelve (the minor prophets).
iii. The Writings Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Solomon, Ruth, Lamentations, Ecclesiastes, Esther, Daniel, Ezra, Nehemiah, and Chronicles.

The books of our English Bible are, on the whole, grouped according to their literary character, as follows:
Old Testament
i. Law Genesis to Deuteronomy
ii. Poetry Job to Song of Solomon
iii. Prophecy Isaiah to Malachi

New Testament
i. History Matthew to Acts
ii. Letters Romans to Jude
iii. Prophecy Revelation

6. Their Language
The Old Testament was written in Hebrew except for a few sections which were written in Aramaic. The New Testament was written in Greek and was first translated into English by John Wycliffe in 1382-1388. The King James, or Authorized, Version of the Bible was made in 1611. Organization AY Module
Wednesday, January 03, 2024

Sir/Madam, the candle I now light represents the Organization AY Module.

1. Organization Module is the module which presents the history and organization of youth ministry in the Seventh-day Adventist Church. The strong emphasis is based in the local Church. This module also deals with the responsibilities of Youth Leaders at the local Church.

2. Leadership module is one of the modules which incorporates the aspects of training and actual practice of activities required for senior leadership. This module emphasizes on the qualities of idea leadership, the psychology of leadership, leadership styles and its functions. The practical matters such as how to chair a meeting is also taught under this module.

3. Commitment Module is one which revolves around those activities and programs that are devotional in nature, and that provide for special affirmation and celebration opportunities. Such activities are; “Weeks of Prayer” “Weeks of Spiritual Emphasis” “Bible Conferences” “Morning Watch.” It also talks about the time of reaffirmation of Youth’s continuing faith and confidence in God like the induction ceremonies. This is the module  which dedicated to strengthening the spiritual life of youths and their leaders as they participate in such activities

4. Discipleship module is the module which teaches youths on how to become good disciples and stay committed in that way of life. In this module the extensive presentation on psychological aspect of youth leadership is given. The spiritual wellbeing of all members is emphasized in order for all member to be good disciples. The ultimate objective of this module is to disciple others to the Lord and to equip them to share in this new experience.

5. Worship Module is the module which helps youths to understand that worship is not merely attending church. They understand that religious exercise of worship includes learning to live in God’s presence, and integrating those things learned at church into one’s daily living. This is the module which helps Adventist Youth discover the joy of worship, and how to adopt a worshipful spirit while attending church.

6. Witnessing Module is the module which enables Youths share their own individual experience in the Lord. In this module, Adventist Youth discuss effective witnessing and how it might be accomplished within the Society.

7. Fellowship Module is the module designed to guide Youth Leaders in finding resources to help provide meaningful recreation for the AY Society. In this module Youths learn how to deal with their leisure time. This is module provides and adopt recreations and entertainments that reflect the Adventism lifestyle.

8. Empowering Youth Module is the module that summarizes the whole purpose of Youth Ministry in the Seventh-day Adventist Church. Salvation and Service focused Youth Ministry through Discipleship, Leadership, evangelism. Service or Mission empowers Youths to be strong Christians and participant in the mission of the church. This is the module which combines the eight modules into four strategic themes of Youth Ministry in the Seventh-day Adventist Church
